About this project

The theme “Bones of our Past” has inspired me to write about my trip to Poland this summer. My dad, sister and I went to Poland to explore our heritage. Due to Jewish persecution prior, during and after the second World War, many familial documents were destroyed in an attempt to hide Jewish heritage. I am Jewish. All four of my grandparents endured the Holocaust and are originally from Poland. This portfolio project will chronicle my father’s parents lives prior, during and after the Holocaust in Poland. It will mention important dates and locations. My grandparents were lucky in that they survived, many of their family members did not and perished in concentration camps or locations unknown. For this reason it was very important for me to document and explore my history. After the second World War my grandparents miraculously immigrated to Canada and have been there ever since. However, the second and third generation are ultimately unacquainted with our family history. This project has helped me to trace some of my lineage, and for that I am thankful.

History of Poland

In 1933 1.7% of the total European population was Jewish (9.5 million). This represented more than 60 percent of the world’s population at that time. Poland was home to the second-largest Jewish population, comprised of over three million Jews. Warsaw was comprised of 300,000 Jews (30% of the population of the city). Within the duration of the Second World War, 85% of Polish Jewry was exterminated. Many Jews were deported across Europe to German occupied Poland where they were imprisoned in concentration camps and or murdered. Since the Second World War Poland has not rebuilt the same Jewish community as it once had. Jewish people have occupied Poland for over a thousand years. The initial mass migration was due to detrimental controversies in western and central Europe. Over time, Jewish people gained rights (mainly argued for by the Polish King Kazimierz the Great in the 14th century) who encouraged Jewish settlements in Poland. Jews were a crucial part of Poland’s identity for centuries; the first coins issued in Poland bore Hebrew, not Polish inscriptions. Click here for more information.
